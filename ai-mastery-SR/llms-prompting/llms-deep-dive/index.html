LLMs: Fundamentals & Comparison

What are Large Language Models (LLMs)?

What are Large Language Models (LLMs)?

Large Language Models (LLMs) are advanced artificial intelligence systems designed to understand and generate human language.

They are:

  • “Large” because they contain billions (sometimes trillions) of parameters.

  • “Language models” because they are trained to predict and generate text.


Core Idea Behind LLMs

At their core, LLMs work by predicting the next word (or token) in a sequence.

Example:

Input:
“The capital of France is”

The model predicts:
“Paris”

They learn this ability by being trained on massive amounts of text data such as:

  • Books

  • Websites

  • Articles

  • Code

  • Conversations


How Do LLMs Work?

  1. Tokenization
    Text is broken into smaller units called tokens.

  2. Embeddings
    Tokens are converted into numerical vectors.

  3. Transformer Architecture
    Most modern LLMs use a neural network architecture called the Transformer, which uses attention mechanisms to understand context.

  4. Training Objective
    They are trained to predict the next token given previous tokens.

Example training task:

Input: “Machine learning is”
Target: “a”

Over time, the model learns grammar, facts, reasoning patterns, and structure.


What Makes Them “Large”?

LLMs are large in two main ways:

  1. Number of parameters
    These are the learnable weights of the neural network.

  2. Training data size
    Trained on massive datasets.

Examples:

  • GPT models

  • LLaMA

  • PaLM

  • Claude


Capabilities of LLMs

LLMs can:

  • Answer questions

  • Summarize text

  • Translate languages

  • Generate code

  • Write essays

  • Perform reasoning tasks

  • Extract structured data

  • Engage in conversation

They are general-purpose language systems.


Key Characteristics

  1. Context-aware
    They consider previous words in a sentence.

  2. Pre-trained
    They are trained on large datasets before being used for specific tasks.

  3. Fine-tunable
    They can be adapted for domain-specific tasks.

  4. Probabilistic
    They generate text based on probability distributions.


Limitations

LLMs:

  • Can hallucinate (generate incorrect information)

  • Do not truly “understand” in a human sense

  • May reflect biases from training data

  • Are sensitive to prompt phrasing


In Simple Terms

An LLM is a very large neural network trained on massive amounts of text that can understand, generate, and manipulate human language by predicting the next word in a sequence based on context.


How LLMs are created: LLMs are produced through pretraining on massive text datasets — typically hundreds of billions to trillions of tokens — using next-token prediction as the training objective. This is followed by supervised fine-tuning (SFT) and alignment training (RLHF or DPO) to make the model helpful and safe. The base model's broad capabilities emerge from pretraining; fine-tuning shapes behavior without significantly changing core knowledge. This distinction — base model vs. aligned chat model — is fundamental to understanding LLM behavior.

How are LLMs different from traditional language models?

1. Model Architecture

Traditional language models:

  • Often based on statistical methods.

  • Examples: N-grams, Hidden Markov Models (HMMs).

  • Sometimes small neural networks (RNNs, LSTMs).

LLMs:

  • Based on Transformer architecture.

  • Use self-attention mechanisms.

  • Can process long-range dependencies efficiently.

Major difference:
LLMs use deep Transformer networks, while traditional models rely on simpler statistical or sequential neural methods.


2. Scale (Parameters and Data)

Traditional language models:

  • Small number of parameters.

  • Trained on limited datasets.

  • Often task-specific.

LLMs:

  • Billions or trillions of parameters.

  • Trained on massive internet-scale datasets.

  • Designed to be general-purpose.

Scale is one of the biggest differences.


3. Context Handling

Traditional models (like N-grams):

  • Only look at a small fixed window of previous words.

  • Example: Trigram model looks at last 2 words only.

RNN/LSTM:

  • Can handle sequences but struggle with very long context.

LLMs:

  • Use self-attention.

  • Can capture long-range dependencies across large context windows.

  • Understand relationships between distant words in text.


4. Training Objective

Traditional models:

  • Often built for specific tasks.

  • May require feature engineering.

  • Designed for classification, tagging, or speech modeling.

LLMs:

  • Pretrained on next-token prediction across massive text corpora.

  • Learn general language representations.

  • Can perform many tasks without retraining.


5. Task Adaptability

Traditional language models:

  • Need separate models for each task.

  • Example: One model for translation, another for classification.

LLMs:

  • One model can perform multiple tasks.

  • Can handle:

    • Question answering

    • Summarization

    • Code generation

    • Translation

    • Reasoning

  • Often require only prompting instead of retraining.

This is called in-context learning.


6. Feature Engineering

Traditional NLP systems:

  • Required manual feature engineering.

  • Example: POS tags, TF-IDF, handcrafted rules.

LLMs:

  • Automatically learn representations from raw text.

  • Minimal manual feature engineering needed.


7. Performance

Traditional models:

  • Limited generalization.

  • Struggle with complex reasoning or creative generation.

LLMs:

  • Strong generalization.

  • Capable of reasoning, summarization, and complex language tasks.

  • Perform well across domains.


8. Limitations

Traditional models:

  • Less powerful but more interpretable.

  • Smaller computational cost.

LLMs:

  • Very powerful but computationally expensive.

  • Can hallucinate.

  • Require large infrastructure.


Summary of Key Differences

Traditional Language Models:

  • Statistical or small neural models

  • Limited context window

  • Task-specific

  • Small scale

  • Require feature engineering

LLMs:

  • Transformer-based deep models

  • Large context window

  • General-purpose

  • Massive scale

  • Learn features automatically

  • Capable of in-context learning

In simple terms, LLMs are large-scale Transformer-based models trained on huge datasets, capable of performing many language tasks with a single model, whereas traditional language models are smaller, task-specific, and limited in scope and context handling.

What are Foundation Models?

What are Foundation Models?

Foundation Models are large-scale AI models trained on massive amounts of broad, diverse data that can be adapted to many different downstream tasks.

They are called “foundation” models because they serve as a base or foundation upon which many applications can be built.


Core Idea

Instead of training separate models for every task (classification, translation, summarization, etc.), a single large model is:

  1. Pretrained on massive data (self-supervised learning).

  2. Adapted or prompted for many different tasks.

One model → Many applications.


Key Characteristics of Foundation Models

  1. Large Scale

    • Billions or trillions of parameters.

    • Trained on huge datasets.

  2. Self-Supervised Pretraining

    • Trained without manual labels.

    • Example objective: predict the next word.

  3. General-Purpose

    • Can perform multiple tasks.

    • Not limited to a single application.

  4. Adaptability

    • Can be adapted using:

      • Fine-tuning

      • Prompting

      • Few-shot learning

      • Retrieval augmentation

  5. Transfer Learning Capability

    • Knowledge learned during pretraining transfers to new tasks.


Examples of Foundation Models

Text-based:

  • GPT models

  • LLaMA

  • PaLM

  • Claude

Vision-based:

  • Vision Transformers (ViT)

  • CLIP

Multimodal:

  • GPT-4 (text + image)

  • Gemini

  • Flamingo


Difference Between Foundation Models and Traditional Models

Traditional Model:

  • Built for one task.

  • Trained on task-specific labeled data.

  • Example: Sentiment classifier trained only for sentiment analysis.

Foundation Model:

  • Trained once on massive general data.

  • Adapted for many tasks.

  • Example: Same model can do translation, summarization, coding, reasoning.


Why Are They Important?

  1. Reduce need for labeled data.

  2. Reduce need to build models from scratch.

  3. Enable rapid application development.

  4. Power modern AI systems like chatbots, copilots, and generative AI tools.


Simple Definition

A Foundation Model is a large, general-purpose model trained on massive datasets using self-supervised learning, which can be adapted to perform a wide range of tasks across domains.

What is tokenization, and why is it important in LLMs?

What is Tokenization?

Tokenization is the process of breaking text into smaller units called tokens so that a language model can process them.

LLMs do not understand raw text directly.
They operate on numbers.

So the pipeline is:

Text → Tokens → Token IDs → Vectors → Model processing


What is a Token?

A token is not always a full word.

Depending on the tokenizer, a token can be:

  • A word

  • Part of a word (subword)

  • A character

  • A punctuation mark

Example:

Sentence:
“ChatGPT is powerful.”

Possible tokens:
["Chat", "GPT", " is", " powerful", "."]

Or using subword tokenization:
["Chat", "G", "PT", " is", " power", "ful", "."]


Types of Tokenization

  1. Word-level tokenization
    Splits text by spaces.
    Limitation: Cannot handle unknown words well.

  2. Character-level tokenization
    Each character is a token.
    Too long and inefficient.

  3. Subword tokenization (most common in LLMs)
    Uses algorithms like:

    • Byte Pair Encoding (BPE)

    • WordPiece

    • SentencePiece

Subword tokenization balances vocabulary size and flexibility.

Example:
“unbelievable”
May be split into:
["un", "believ", "able"]

This allows models to understand unseen words.


Why is Tokenization Important in LLMs?

  1. Converts text into numerical form
    Neural networks work only with numbers.

  2. Controls vocabulary size
    Instead of storing millions of full words, models use subword units.

  3. Handles rare or unknown words
    New words can be broken into known subwords.

  4. Affects model efficiency
    Better tokenization reduces sequence length.

  5. Determines context window usage
    LLMs have token limits (e.g., 8K, 32K tokens).
    More tokens → more memory usage.


Example of Token Limit Importance

If a model has a 4,000-token limit:

  • Shorter tokenization → More usable text.

  • Inefficient tokenization → Fewer words fit in context.


Tokenization in Practice

When you send text to an LLM:

  1. Text is tokenized.

  2. Each token is mapped to an integer ID.

  3. IDs are converted to embeddings.

  4. Model predicts next token ID.

  5. IDs are converted back to text.


Why Subword Tokenization Is Powerful

It allows:

  • Handling typos

  • Handling new words

  • Supporting multiple languages

  • Reducing vocabulary size

  • Better generalization

What role do embeddings play in LLMs?

What are Embeddings?

Embeddings are numerical vector representations of tokens (or words, sentences, documents) that capture their meaning.

Since neural networks cannot understand text directly, words must be converted into numbers. Embeddings are those meaningful numerical representations.


Why Not Just Use Token IDs?

When text is tokenized, each token is assigned an integer ID.

Example:
“cat” → 1052
“dog” → 3481

These numbers are arbitrary and do not carry meaning.

The model cannot infer that “cat” and “dog” are similar just from token IDs.

Embeddings solve this problem.


What Do Embeddings Do?

Embeddings map tokens into dense vectors in high-dimensional space.

Example:

“cat” → [0.21, -0.45, 0.88, …]
“dog” → [0.19, -0.40, 0.85, …]

If two words have similar meanings, their embeddings will be close in vector space.

Distance in vector space ≈ similarity in meaning.


Role of Embeddings in LLMs

  1. Represent Meaning

Embeddings capture semantic relationships.

Example:
king − man + woman ≈ queen

This works because embeddings encode relationships between words.


  1. Provide Input to the Model

Pipeline:

Text → Tokens → Token IDs → Embeddings → Transformer layers

Embeddings are the actual input to the neural network.

Without embeddings, the model cannot process language meaningfully.


  1. Capture Context (Contextual Embeddings)

In modern LLMs, embeddings are contextual.

The word “bank” in:

“I deposited money in the bank.”
vs
“I sat near the river bank.”

will have different embeddings depending on context.

The Transformer architecture refines embeddings layer by layer.


  1. Enable Similarity Search

Embeddings are used for:

  • Semantic search

  • Document retrieval

  • Clustering

  • Recommendation systems

  • Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG)

If two sentences are similar, their embeddings will be close.


  1. Enable Transfer Learning

Pretrained embeddings already capture rich language knowledge.

When fine-tuning, the model adjusts embeddings for specific tasks.


Types of Embeddings

  1. Token embeddings
    Represent individual tokens.

  2. Positional embeddings
    Encode word order information.

  3. Sentence embeddings
    Represent entire sentences.

  4. Document embeddings
    Represent larger chunks of text.


Why Embeddings Are Critical

Embeddings:

  • Convert language into mathematical space

  • Capture semantic relationships

  • Allow similarity measurement

  • Serve as the foundation for attention mechanisms

Without embeddings, LLMs would only see arbitrary numbers, not meaningful representations.

How do LLMs handle out-of-vocabulary (OOV) words?

What is an Out-of-Vocabulary (OOV) Word?

An out-of-vocabulary word is a word that was not explicitly seen during training or is not present as a full word in the model’s vocabulary.

Examples:

  • Newly invented words

  • Misspellings

  • Rare technical terms

  • Slang

  • Foreign words

Traditional word-level models struggled heavily with OOV words because they relied on fixed word dictionaries.


How LLMs Handle OOV Words

Modern LLMs rarely face true OOV problems because they use subword tokenization instead of word-level tokenization.

They break unknown words into smaller known pieces.


1. Subword Tokenization (Main Solution)

LLMs use algorithms like:

  • Byte Pair Encoding (BPE)

  • WordPiece

  • SentencePiece

Instead of storing entire words, they store smaller fragments.

Example:

Word:
“unhappiness”

May be split into:
["un", "happi", "ness"]

Even if “unhappiness” never appeared in training, its subwords likely did.

So the model can still process it.


2. Handling Rare or New Words

Example:
“ChatGPTization”

Tokenized as:
["Chat", "G", "PT", "ization"]

Even though the word is new, the parts are known.

This prevents complete failure on unseen words.


3. Character-Level Fallback (Indirectly)

Because subword tokenization operates at a smaller granularity, even rare spellings like:

“qwertyuiop”

Can be broken into:

["q", "wer", "ty", "ui", "op"] (depending on tokenizer)

Thus, almost any word can be decomposed.


4. Byte-Level Tokenization

Some tokenizers operate at the byte level.

This guarantees that any string (including emojis or unknown characters) can be represented.

Example:
Even unusual symbols can be tokenized into byte sequences.

This makes true OOV cases extremely rare in modern LLMs.


Why This Is Important

Without subword tokenization:

  • Unknown words would map to a generic token.

  • Model would lose specific meaning.

  • Performance would drop.

Modern LLMs avoid using a single unknown token by decomposing words into meaningful pieces.


Practical Example

Sentence:
“He invested in cryptocurrency.”

Even if “cryptocurrency” is rare, it may split into:

["crypto", "currency"]

The model understands both parts and infers meaning.


Summary

LLMs handle OOV words using subword tokenization. Instead of failing on unseen words, they break them into smaller known tokens. This allows them to process rare, new, misspelled, or domain-specific words effectively without needing a fixed dictionary of full words.

What are Sequence-to-Sequence (Seq2Seq) Models?

What are Sequence-to-Sequence (Seq2Seq) Models?

Sequence-to-Sequence (Seq2Seq) models are neural network architectures designed to transform one sequence into another sequence.

Input: A sequence
Output: A sequence

The input and output sequences can have:

  • Different lengths

  • Different meanings

  • Different vocabularies


Why Are Seq2Seq Models Needed?

Many real-world problems involve mapping one sequence to another:

  • Machine translation

  • Text summarization

  • Speech recognition

  • Question answering

  • Chatbots

Example:

English:
“How are you?”

French:
“Comment allez-vous ?”

The input and output are both sequences of words, but they differ in structure and length.


Basic Architecture of Seq2Seq

Traditional Seq2Seq models consist of two main components:

  1. Encoder

  2. Decoder

Structure:

Input Sequence → Encoder → Context Vector → Decoder → Output Sequence


1. Encoder

The encoder:

  • Reads the input sequence step by step.

  • Converts it into a fixed-length representation called a context vector.

If using RNN/LSTM:

At each time step, the hidden state updates based on input and previous hidden state.

Final hidden state = context vector

This context vector summarizes the entire input sequence.


2. Decoder

The decoder:

  • Takes the context vector.

  • Generates output tokens one by one.

  • Each generated token becomes input for the next step.

At each step:

  • Predict next word.

  • Use predicted word as input.

  • Continue until end token is produced.


Problem with Basic Seq2Seq

The original Seq2Seq model compressed the entire input into one fixed-length vector.

For long sentences:

  • Information gets lost.

  • Performance drops.


Attention Mechanism (Improvement)

Attention allows the decoder to look at different parts of the input sequence dynamically instead of relying on one fixed context vector.

Instead of:
Single context vector

It uses:
Weighted combination of encoder outputs at each decoding step.

This improves performance significantly.


Modern Seq2Seq Models

Modern models use Transformers instead of RNNs.

Transformer-based Seq2Seq architecture:

  • Encoder (self-attention layers)

  • Decoder (self-attention + cross-attention)

Examples:

  • T5

  • BART

  • Machine translation models

  • Many LLM-based encoder-decoder systems


Example: Machine Translation

Input:
“I love programming.”

Encoder processes entire sentence.

Decoder generates:
“J’aime programmer.”

Each output word is generated conditioned on:

  • Previous output words

  • Input sequence representation


Key Characteristics

  1. Handles variable-length input and output

  2. Used for transformation tasks

  3. Learns conditional probability:
    P(Output | Input)


Difference from Autoencoder

Autoencoder:
Input = Output
Goal: Reconstruction

Seq2Seq:
Input ≠ Output
Goal: Transformation


Summary

Sequence-to-Sequence models are neural network architectures that convert one sequence into another using an encoder-decoder structure. They are widely used in tasks like translation, summarization, and conversational AI, and are often enhanced with attention mechanisms or built using Transformers.

What is the difference between an encoder and a decoder?

An encoder is a neural network component that reads and processes input data and converts it into a meaningful internal representation.

Its main job is:

Input → Compressed / Encoded Representation

In sequence models (like translation):

  • The encoder reads the entire input sequence.

  • It converts it into hidden representations.

  • These representations capture meaning, structure, and context.

Example:

Input:
“I love machine learning.”

The encoder transforms this sentence into numerical representations that capture its meaning.


What is a Decoder?

A decoder is a neural network component that takes an encoded representation and generates an output sequence.

Its main job is:

Encoded Representation → Output Sequence

In translation:

  • The decoder receives the encoded meaning.

  • It generates output one token at a time.

  • Each output token depends on:

    • The encoded input

    • Previously generated tokens

Example:

Encoded representation →
“J’aime l’apprentissage automatique.”


Core Functional Difference

Encoder:

  • Understands and compresses input.

  • Extracts features or meaning.

  • Does not generate final output directly.

Decoder:

  • Generates output sequence.

  • Uses encoded information.

  • Produces tokens step by step.


In Transformer Architecture

Encoder:

  • Uses self-attention.

  • Processes entire input simultaneously.

  • Produces contextual embeddings.

Decoder:

  • Uses self-attention (on generated tokens).

  • Uses cross-attention (to encoder outputs).

  • Predicts next token.


Key Differences

  1. Purpose

Encoder:
Extract and represent information.

Decoder:
Generate output from representation.


  1. Direction

Encoder:
Input → Representation

Decoder:
Representation → Output


  1. Usage in Different Models

Encoder-only models:

  • BERT

  • Used for classification, embeddings, retrieval.

Decoder-only models:

  • GPT

  • Used for text generation.

Encoder-Decoder models:

  • T5

  • BART

  • Used for translation, summarization.


Example Comparison

Task: Translate English to French

Encoder:
Reads English sentence.

Decoder:
Generates French sentence.

How do autoregressive models differ from masked models?

1. Autoregressive Models

Autoregressive models generate text one token at a time, predicting the next token based on previous tokens.

Training objective:

Predict next token given previous tokens.

Mathematically:

P(x₁, x₂, x₃, …, xₙ)
= P(x₁) × P(x₂ | x₁) × P(x₃ | x₁, x₂) × …

Example:

Input:
“The capital of France is”

Model predicts:
“Paris”

Then continues predicting the next word after “Paris”.

Key characteristics:

  • Left-to-right generation

  • Causal masking (cannot see future tokens)

  • Naturally suited for text generation

  • Used in chatbots and content generation

Examples:

  • GPT models

  • LLaMA

  • Claude (decoder-only models)


2. Masked Language Models (Masked Models)

Masked models are trained to predict missing words inside a sentence.

Training objective:

Randomly mask some tokens and predict them using surrounding context.

Example training input:

“The capital of [MASK] is Paris.”

Model predicts:
“France”

Key characteristics:

  • Uses bidirectional context (left and right)

  • Sees entire sentence except masked token

  • Not inherently generative (cannot naturally generate long text step-by-step)

  • Strong at understanding and classification tasks

Examples:

  • BERT

  • RoBERTa

  • ALBERT


3. Core Differences

Training Objective

Autoregressive:
Predict next token sequentially.

Masked:
Predict masked tokens within a full sequence.


Context Usage

Autoregressive:
Can only see past tokens.

Masked:
Can see both left and right context.


Primary Use Case

Autoregressive:
Text generation

  • Chatbots

  • Story writing

  • Code generation

Masked:
Text understanding

  • Classification

  • Named entity recognition

  • Sentence similarity


Generation Ability

Autoregressive:
Naturally generates text token by token.

Masked:
Not designed for free-form generation.


Architecture Style

Autoregressive:
Typically decoder-only Transformers.

Masked:
Typically encoder-only Transformers.


4. Intuition

Autoregressive model:
Writes a sentence like a human typing from left to right.

Masked model:
Reads the whole sentence and fills in missing blanks.


5. Summary

Autoregressive models generate text sequentially by predicting the next token based on previous tokens. They are ideal for generative tasks.

Masked models predict missing tokens using full bidirectional context. They are ideal for language understanding tasks.

Autoregressive = Generation-focused
Masked = Understanding-focused

What is Next Sentence Prediction (NSP)?

What is Next Sentence Prediction (NSP)?

Next Sentence Prediction (NSP) is a training objective used during the pretraining of some language models, most notably BERT.

Its purpose is to help the model understand the relationship between two sentences.


Core Idea

In NSP, the model is given two sentences:

Sentence A
Sentence B

The task is to predict whether Sentence B logically follows Sentence A in the original text.

The model outputs:

  • “IsNext” (Sentence B follows Sentence A)

  • “NotNext” (Sentence B is unrelated)


Example

Case 1: Correct Next Sentence

Sentence A:
“I went to the store to buy groceries.”

Sentence B:
“I bought milk and bread.”

Label: IsNext


Case 2: Random Sentence

Sentence A:
“I went to the store to buy groceries.”

Sentence B:
“Mount Everest is the highest mountain in the world.”

Label: NotNext

The model must learn whether the second sentence logically continues the first.


How NSP Is Trained

During pretraining:

  • 50% of the time, Sentence B is the actual next sentence.

  • 50% of the time, Sentence B is randomly sampled from the dataset.

The model learns to distinguish coherent sentence pairs from unrelated ones.


Why NSP Was Introduced

NSP was designed to help models understand:

  • Sentence relationships

  • Discourse structure

  • Coherence across paragraphs

  • Context flow

This was especially useful for tasks like:

  • Question answering

  • Natural language inference

  • Dialogue systems


Where NSP Was Used

NSP was used in:

  • BERT (original paper)

However, later research found that NSP did not always significantly improve performance.

Some models removed it:

  • RoBERTa removed NSP and achieved better results by focusing more on masked language modeling.


Limitations of NSP

  • May not strongly improve all tasks.

  • Sometimes replaced by better training objectives.

  • Some research suggests it was not critical to BERT’s success.


Summary

Next Sentence Prediction is a pretraining task where the model learns to determine whether one sentence logically follows another. It was used in BERT to help the model understand sentence relationships and discourse coherence.

How does the Transformer architecture overcome challenges of traditional Seq2Seq models?

1. Problems with Traditional Seq2Seq Models (RNN/LSTM-based)

Traditional Seq2Seq models used RNNs or LSTMs as encoder and decoder. They had several limitations:

  1. Sequential Processing

    • RNNs process tokens one by one.

    • Cannot parallelize training efficiently.

    • Training is slow.

  2. Long-Term Dependency Problem

    • Hard to remember information from far earlier in the sequence.

    • Even LSTMs struggle with very long sentences.

  3. Fixed-Length Context Vector (Early Seq2Seq)

    • Entire input compressed into a single vector.

    • Information bottleneck for long sequences.

  4. Vanishing/Exploding Gradients

    • Deep RNNs suffer from unstable gradients.

  5. Limited Parallelization

    • Each time step depends on previous one.

    • Not GPU-friendly.


2. How Transformers Overcome These Challenges

The Transformer architecture replaces recurrence with self-attention mechanisms.


A. Self-Attention Instead of Recurrence

Instead of processing words sequentially, Transformers:

  • Look at all tokens simultaneously.

  • Compute relationships between every pair of tokens.

Example:

Sentence:
“The cat that chased the mouse was hungry.”

The word “was” can directly attend to “cat” even though they are far apart.

This solves long-range dependency issues.


B. Parallel Processing

RNN:
Processes token 1 → then token 2 → then token 3.

Transformer:
Processes all tokens at the same time.

This allows:

  • Faster training

  • Better GPU utilization

  • Scalability to massive datasets


C. No Fixed-Length Bottleneck

Early Seq2Seq models compressed the entire sentence into one vector.

Transformer with attention:

  • Each decoding step can attend to all encoder outputs.

  • No single bottleneck vector.

This improves translation quality for long sentences.


D. Better Long-Term Dependency Handling

Self-attention creates direct connections between distant words.

Distance between tokens becomes 1 attention step instead of many RNN steps.

This makes learning long dependencies much easier.


E. Stable Gradient Flow

Transformers:

  • Avoid deep recurrence chains.

  • Use residual connections.

  • Use layer normalization.

This improves training stability.


F. Scalable Architecture

Transformers scale well in:

  • Number of layers

  • Model size

  • Training data

  • Parallel computation

This scalability enabled Large Language Models.


3. Key Components That Enable These Improvements

  1. Self-Attention
    Captures relationships across entire sequence.

  2. Multi-Head Attention
    Learns multiple types of relationships simultaneously.

  3. Positional Encoding
    Adds order information (since no recurrence).

  4. Feedforward Layers
    Add non-linear transformation.

  5. Residual Connections
    Help deep training.


4. Practical Impact

Transformers dramatically improved:

  • Machine translation

  • Text summarization

  • Question answering

  • Large-scale generative models

Modern LLMs are all Transformer-based.


5. Summary

Traditional Seq2Seq models struggled with long-term dependencies, sequential computation, and information bottlenecks.

Transformers overcome these issues by:

  • Replacing recurrence with self-attention

This architectural shift made modern large language models possible.

What are positional encodings in LLMs?

Why Do We Need Positional Encodings?

Transformers process all tokens in parallel using self-attention.

Unlike RNNs, they do not inherently understand word order because:

  • Self-attention treats input as a set of tokens.

  • There is no built-in concept of sequence position.

Without position information:

“The dog bit the man.”
“The man bit the dog.”

Would look identical to the model (same tokens, different order).

To solve this, Transformers use positional encodings.


What Are Positional Encodings?

Positional encodings are numerical representations added to token embeddings to give the model information about the position of each token in a sequence.

Pipeline:

Text → Tokenization → Token Embeddings
Then:
Token Embedding + Positional Encoding → Input to Transformer

This allows the model to know:

  • Which word comes first

  • Relative positions

  • Distance between words


How Positional Encodings Work

Each token embedding is combined with a position vector.

If:

Embedding(token) = E
Position vector = P

Final input representation:

E + P

This enriched vector now contains:

  • Meaning information (from embedding)

  • Order information (from position encoding)


Types of Positional Encodings

1. Absolute Positional Encoding (Original Transformer)

In the original Transformer paper, positional encodings were fixed sinusoidal functions.

They used sine and cosine functions of different frequencies.

Why sine and cosine?

  • Allows the model to learn relative positions.

  • Generalizes to longer sequences.

  • Smooth and continuous.

Formula (conceptually):

PE(pos, 2i) = sin(pos / 10000^(2i/d_model))
PE(pos, 2i+1) = cos(pos / 10000^(2i/d_model))

You do not need to memorize the formula — just know that:

  • Each position gets a unique pattern.

  • Nearby positions have similar encodings.

  • Relative distances can be computed.


2. Learned Positional Embeddings

Instead of fixed functions, the model learns positional vectors during training.

Each position has a learnable embedding:

Position 1 → Vector A
Position 2 → Vector B
Position 3 → Vector C

These are optimized during training.

Used in many modern models.


3. Relative Positional Encoding

Instead of encoding absolute position (position 5, position 6), the model encodes relative distance between tokens.

This improves:

  • Long sequence modeling

  • Generalization

  • Efficiency

Used in more advanced Transformer variants.


Why Positional Encoding Is Important

  1. Preserves word order
    Without it, sentence structure is lost.

  2. Enables syntax understanding
    Word order determines meaning.

  3. Supports long-range dependency modeling
    Model understands how far apart words are.

  4. Makes Transformers sequence-aware
    Without recurrence, this is essential.


Example

Sentence:
“I ate pizza yesterday.”

Each word embedding is combined with its position:

“I” → Embedding + Position 1
“ate” → Embedding + Position 2
“pizza” → Embedding + Position 3
“yesterday” → Embedding + Position 4

Now the model understands order.


Summary

Positional encodings are numerical vectors added to token embeddings in Transformers to inject information about word order. Since Transformers process tokens in parallel and have no inherent sense of sequence, positional encodings allow them to understand the structure and order of language.

What is attention in Transformer models?

What is Attention in Transformer Models?

Attention is a mechanism that allows a model to focus on the most relevant parts of the input when processing a token.

Instead of treating all words equally, attention assigns different importance (weights) to different words in a sentence.

In simple terms:

Attention tells the model “which words should I pay more attention to when understanding this word?”


Why Is Attention Needed?

In language, meaning depends on relationships between words.

Example:

“The cat that chased the mouse was hungry.”

The word “was” relates to “cat,” not “mouse.”

A model needs a way to connect related words, even if they are far apart.

Attention allows this.


Core Idea of Self-Attention

In Transformers, attention is usually self-attention.

Self-attention means:

Each word looks at all other words in the same sentence to understand context.

For every token:

  • It calculates how important every other token is.

  • It assigns weights.

  • It builds a new contextual representation.


How Attention Works (Conceptual Steps)

For each token:

  1. Create three vectors:

    • Query (Q)

    • Key (K)

    • Value (V)

  2. Compare Query of current word with Keys of all words.

  3. Compute similarity scores (dot product).

  4. Apply softmax to turn scores into probabilities (attention weights).

  5. Multiply weights with Value vectors.

  6. Sum them to get final output representation.

So:

Output = Weighted sum of Value vectors

The weights reflect importance.


Example

Sentence:
“I deposited money in the bank.”

For the word “bank”:

Attention may focus more on:

  • “money”

  • “deposited”

Less on:

  • “I”

This helps determine that “bank” refers to a financial institution, not a riverbank.


What Is Scaled Dot-Product Attention?

In Transformers, attention is computed as:

Attention(Q, K, V) = softmax(QKᵀ / √d_k) V

Where:

  • Q = Queries

  • K = Keys

  • V = Values

  • d_k = dimension scaling factor

The scaling prevents extremely large values during training.


What Is Multi-Head Attention?

Instead of performing attention once, Transformers do it multiple times in parallel.

Each “head” learns different relationships:

  • One head may focus on grammar.

  • One on long-distance relationships.

  • One on coreference.

  • One on syntax.

Then results are combined.

This makes attention more powerful.


Why Attention Is Powerful

  1. Captures long-range dependencies
    Any word can directly connect to any other word.

  2. Fully parallelizable
    Unlike RNNs, no sequential dependency.

  3. Context-aware representations
    Each word gets a representation based on entire sentence.

  4. Scales well
    Enables large models like GPT.


Types of Attention in Transformers

  1. Self-Attention
    Words attend to other words in the same sequence.

  2. Cross-Attention
    Used in encoder-decoder models.
    Decoder attends to encoder outputs.

  3. Causal (Masked) Attention
    Used in autoregressive models.
    Prevents attending to future tokens.

What is Multi-head attention?

What is Multi-Head Attention?

Multi-head attention is an extension of the attention mechanism used in Transformers where multiple attention operations are performed in parallel, and their results are combined.

Instead of computing attention once, the model computes it multiple times (in parallel), each time focusing on different aspects of the input.


Why Do We Need Multi-Head Attention?

A single attention mechanism can learn relationships between words, but it may focus on only one type of relationship at a time.

Language contains multiple types of relationships:

  • Grammatical relationships

  • Subject–verb agreement

  • Long-range dependencies

  • Coreference (e.g., “John said he…”)

  • Semantic similarity

Multi-head attention allows the model to capture different types of relationships simultaneously.


How Multi-Head Attention Works

For each attention head:

  1. The input embeddings are linearly projected into:

    • Query (Q)

    • Key (K)

    • Value (V)

  2. Each head computes scaled dot-product attention independently:

    Attention(Q, K, V)

  3. Each head produces its own output.

  4. Outputs of all heads are:

    • Concatenated

    • Passed through a final linear layer

So:

MultiHead(Q, K, V)
= Concat(head₁, head₂, …, headₕ) × Wₒ

Where:

  • h = number of heads

  • Wₒ = output projection matrix


Intuition Behind Multi-Head Attention

Think of it like multiple “experts” analyzing the same sentence.

Example sentence:

“The boy who was playing football is tired.”

Different heads may focus on:

  • Head 1: “boy” ↔ “is” (subject-verb agreement)

  • Head 2: “boy” ↔ “who”

  • Head 3: “playing” ↔ “football”

  • Head 4: Long-distance dependency between “boy” and “tired”

Each head learns a different attention pattern.


Why Not Just One Big Attention?

Instead of using one large attention mechanism, multi-head attention:

  • Splits the embedding dimension into smaller subspaces.

  • Allows learning different representations in different subspaces.

  • Improves model expressiveness.

  • Makes learning more stable.

Each head operates on a smaller dimensional space (d_model / h), which keeps computational cost manageable.


Advantages of Multi-Head Attention

  1. Captures multiple relationships simultaneously

  2. Improves representation richness

  3. Enables modeling complex dependencies

  4. Scales efficiently in large models

  5. Improves overall performance in NLP tasks


Where Multi-Head Attention Is Used

  • Encoder self-attention

  • Decoder self-attention

  • Encoder–decoder cross-attention

  • All modern LLMs (GPT, BERT, T5, etc.)

How are attention scores computed in transformers?

How Are Attention Scores Computed in Transformers?

Attention scores determine how much one token should focus on another token in a sequence.

In Transformers, attention scores are computed using the Scaled Dot-Product Attention mechanism.


Step 1: Create Query, Key, and Value Vectors

For each token embedding ( X ), three different linear projections are applied:

  • Query vector (Q)

  • Key vector (K)

  • Value vector (V)

These are computed as:

Q = XW_Q
K = XW_K
V = XW_V

Where:

  • ( W_Q, W_K, W_V ) are learnable weight matrices.

  • Each token now has its own Q, K, V vectors.


Step 2: Compute Raw Attention Scores

To determine how much token i attends to token j:

  • Take the dot product of Query of token i and Key of token j.

Mathematically:

Score(i, j) = Q_i · K_j

This dot product measures similarity.

Higher score → more relevant
Lower score → less relevant

For an entire sequence, this becomes:

Scores = QKᵀ

If sequence length = n, this produces an n × n matrix.

Each row represents how one token attends to all tokens.


Step 3: Scale the Scores

The dot product values can become very large if the dimension of Q and K is large.

To stabilize gradients, scores are scaled:

Scaled Scores = (QKᵀ) / √d_k

Where:

  • d_k = dimension of Key vectors

  • √d_k prevents large variance

This scaling improves training stability.


Step 4: Apply Softmax

The scaled scores are converted into probabilities using softmax:

Attention Weights = softmax((QKᵀ) / √d_k)

Softmax ensures:

  • All values are positive

  • Each row sums to 1

Now each row represents attention distribution for a token.


Step 5: Compute Weighted Sum of Values

The final output is computed by multiplying attention weights with Value vectors:

Output = Attention Weights × V

This produces a weighted combination of value vectors.

Tokens that received higher attention scores contribute more to the final representation.


Full Formula

Attention(Q, K, V) = softmax((QKᵀ) / √d_k) V


Intuition

For each token:

  1. Ask a question (Query)

  2. Compare it with all keys (similarity)

  3. Convert similarity into probabilities

  4. Combine values using those probabilities

The result is a context-aware representation of the token.


Example (Conceptual)

Sentence:
“The cat sat on the mat.”

When processing “sat”:

  • Query from “sat”

  • Keys from all words

  • Dot products computed

  • Highest attention may go to:

    • “cat”

    • “on”

The output representation for “sat” becomes a weighted mixture of related words.


In Multi-Head Attention

The entire attention computation is repeated multiple times in parallel with different learned projections.

Each head computes:

Attention(Q_h, K_h, V_h)

Outputs are concatenated and linearly transformed.

This allows the model to capture different types of relationships simultaneously.


Key Points

  • Attention scores are computed using dot products between Query and Key vectors.

  • Scores are scaled for stability.

  • Softmax converts them into probabilities.

  • Final output is a weighted sum of Value vectors.

  • This mechanism allows each token to dynamically gather context from the entire sequence.

What is the softmax function and why is it used in attention?

What is the Softmax Function?

Softmax is a mathematical function that converts a vector of real numbers into a probability distribution.

Given a vector:

z = [z₁, z₂, z₃, ..., zₙ]

Softmax transforms it into:

Softmax(zᵢ) = exp(zᵢ) / Σ exp(zⱼ)

Where:

  • exp() is the exponential function

  • The denominator is the sum of exponentials of all elements

After applying softmax:

  • All values become positive

  • All values sum to 1

  • Each value can be interpreted as a probability


Example

Suppose raw scores are:

[2.0, 1.0, 0.1]

After softmax, they may become approximately:

[0.66, 0.24, 0.10]

The largest value gets the highest probability, but smaller values still retain some weight.


Why Is Softmax Used in Attention?

In attention, we first compute similarity scores:

Scores = QKᵀ / √d_k

These scores can be:

  • Positive or negative

  • Large or small

  • Not normalized

We cannot directly use them as weights.

Softmax solves this problem.


1. Converts Scores into Probabilities

Softmax ensures:

  • All attention weights are positive

  • They sum to 1

This allows attention to behave like a weighted average.

Each token distributes its attention across all tokens.


2. Emphasizes Important Tokens

Because of the exponential function:

  • Larger scores become significantly larger

  • Smaller scores shrink further

This sharpens the distribution.

Important tokens get higher attention.
Less relevant tokens get very small weights.


3. Stabilizes Training

Without normalization:

  • Attention scores could be arbitrarily large

  • Weighted sums could explode

  • Training would be unstable

Softmax normalizes values and keeps gradients well-behaved.


4. Enables Differentiability

Softmax is smooth and differentiable.

This allows:

  • Gradients to flow during backpropagation

  • The model to learn attention patterns effectively


What Happens Without Softmax?

If raw scores were used directly:

  • Some weights could be negative

  • Values would not sum to 1

  • No probabilistic interpretation

  • Training instability

Softmax ensures attention behaves like a probability distribution over tokens.


In Attention Formula

Attention(Q, K, V) = softmax((QKᵀ) / √d_k) V

Softmax converts similarity scores into normalized attention weights, which are then used to compute a weighted sum of Value vectors.


Key Role in Attention

Softmax transforms similarity scores into meaningful, normalized attention weights that:

  • Highlight important tokens

  • Suppress irrelevant ones

  • Maintain numerical stability

  • Enable learning through gradients

How is dot product used in self-attention?

Role of Dot Product in Self-Attention

In self-attention, the dot product is used to measure how similar or relevant one token is to another.

Specifically, it measures the similarity between:

  • The Query vector of one token

  • The Key vector of another token

This similarity becomes the attention score.


Step 1: From Embeddings to Q, K, V

For each token embedding ( X ), we compute:

Q = XW_Q
K = XW_K
V = XW_V

Each token now has its own:

  • Query vector (Q)

  • Key vector (K)

  • Value vector (V)


Step 2: Computing Attention Scores Using Dot Product

To compute how much token i should attend to token j:

Score(i, j) = Q_i · K_j

This is a dot product.

The dot product does two things:

  1. Multiplies corresponding elements

  2. Sums them up

Mathematically:

Q_i · K_j = Σ (Q_i[k] × K_j[k])

If the vectors point in similar directions → large positive value
If not aligned → small or negative value

So, dot product acts as a similarity measure.


Step 3: Building the Attention Matrix

For a sequence of length n:

We compute dot products between:

Each Query vector and all Key vectors.

In matrix form:

Scores = QKᵀ

This produces an n × n matrix:

  • Row i → how token i attends to all tokens

  • Column j → how much attention token j receives


Step 4: Scaling

To prevent very large values (especially in high dimensions), we scale:

Scaled Scores = (QKᵀ) / √d_k

Where:

  • d_k = dimension of Key vectors

This stabilizes training.


Step 5: Convert to Probabilities

Softmax is applied to the scaled dot products:

Attention Weights = softmax((QKᵀ) / √d_k)

These weights determine how much each token contributes.


Step 6: Weighted Sum

Final output:

Attention Output = Attention Weights × V

The dot product determined which tokens matter.
The weighted sum builds the new representation.


Intuition

If two words are related, their Query and Key vectors will align.

Example:

Sentence:
“The cat sat on the mat.”

When processing “sat”:

  • Its Query vector will have strong dot product with:

    • “cat”

    • “on”

  • Smaller dot product with unrelated words.

So attention focuses on meaningful context.


Why Dot Product?

  1. Efficient to compute

  2. Works well in high-dimensional space

  3. Easily parallelizable (matrix multiplication)

  4. Differentiable for backpropagation

It converts semantic similarity into numerical attention strength.


Key Idea

The dot product in self-attention computes similarity between Query and Key vectors. Higher similarity results in higher attention weight, allowing each token to focus on the most relevant parts of the sequence.

What is beam search, and how does it differ from greedy decoding?

What Is Decoding in Language Models?

When a language model generates text, it predicts one token at a time. At each step, it produces a probability distribution over the vocabulary.

Decoding is the strategy used to choose the next token from that distribution.

Two common decoding strategies are:

  • Greedy decoding

  • Beam search


Greedy Decoding

Greedy decoding selects the token with the highest probability at each step.

Process:

  1. Start with input prompt.

  2. Compute probability distribution for next token.

  3. Pick the highest-probability token.

  4. Append it to the sequence.

  5. Repeat until end token or max length.

It always makes the locally optimal choice.

Example

Suppose the model predicts:

Step 1 probabilities:

  • “I” → 0.6

  • “We” → 0.3

  • “They” → 0.1

Greedy picks: “I”

Step 2 probabilities:

  • “am” → 0.5

  • “was” → 0.4

  • “will” → 0.1

Greedy picks: “am”

And continues like this.

Limitation

Greedy decoding may miss better overall sequences.

It chooses the best word now, but that might lead to a worse sentence later.

This is called a locally optimal but globally suboptimal decision.


Beam Search

Beam search keeps multiple possible sequences at each step instead of just one.

It introduces a parameter called beam width (k).

Beam width = number of sequences kept at each step.

How Beam Search Works

  1. Start with input prompt.

  2. At step 1:

    • Select top k most probable tokens.

  3. At step 2:

    • Expand each of the k sequences.

    • Compute probabilities for all possible next tokens.

    • Keep top k total sequences based on cumulative probability.

  4. Repeat until completion.

Instead of tracking 1 sequence, it tracks k sequences.


Example (Beam Width = 2)

Step 1:
Top 2 tokens:

  • “I” (0.6)

  • “We” (0.3)

Step 2:
Expand both:

“I am” → 0.6 × 0.5 = 0.30
“I was” → 0.6 × 0.4 = 0.24
“We are” → 0.3 × 0.6 = 0.18
“We were” → 0.3 × 0.3 = 0.09

Keep top 2:

  • “I am”

  • “I was”

Continue expanding.

This increases chances of finding better overall sequence.


Key Differences

  1. Number of Sequences Tracked

Greedy:
Tracks only 1 sequence.

Beam search:
Tracks multiple sequences (k).


  1. Optimality

Greedy:
Locally optimal at each step.

Beam search:
Closer to globally optimal sequence.


  1. Computation Cost

Greedy:
Fast and simple.

Beam search:
More computationally expensive.


  1. Output Quality

Greedy:
May produce suboptimal text.

Beam search:
Usually produces better, more coherent text.


When to Use Each

Greedy decoding:

  • Fast generation needed

  • Simpler tasks

  • Less computational budget

Beam search:

  • Machine translation

  • Text summarization

  • Tasks where output quality is critical


Limitation of Beam Search

  • Can still miss best sequence.

  • May produce repetitive or generic outputs.

  • Not ideal for creative tasks.

  • Deterministic (less diversity).

That is why for creative generation, sampling methods (top-k, top-p) are often preferred.


Beam search improves over greedy decoding by exploring multiple candidate sequences at each step instead of committing to a single highest-probability token immediately.

Explain the concept of temperature in LLM text generation.

What Is Temperature in LLM Text Generation?

Temperature is a parameter that controls the randomness of token selection during text generation.

It modifies the probability distribution over the next possible tokens before sampling.

LLMs produce a probability distribution like:

P(token₁), P(token₂), P(token₃), ...

Temperature adjusts how sharp or flat this distribution becomes.


How Temperature Works Mathematically

Before sampling, logits (raw model outputs) are divided by temperature:

Adjusted logits = logits / T

Then softmax is applied.

  • If T = 1 → original distribution

  • If T < 1 → sharper distribution

  • If T > 1 → flatter distribution


Effect of Different Temperature Values

1. Low Temperature (e.g., 0.1 – 0.5)

  • Makes high-probability tokens even more likely

  • Suppresses low-probability tokens

  • Output becomes more deterministic

  • Less diversity

  • More predictable responses

Example:

Prompt: “The capital of France is”

Low temperature:
“Paris.”

The model strongly favors the most likely answer.

Used for:

  • Factual answers

  • Code generation

  • Technical explanations

  • Mathematical reasoning


2. Medium Temperature (e.g., 0.7 – 1.0)

  • Balanced randomness

  • Mix of coherence and creativity

  • Common default setting

Used for:

  • General chat

  • Standard content generation


3. High Temperature (e.g., 1.2 and above)

  • Flattens probability distribution

  • Makes lower-probability tokens more likely

  • Increases diversity

  • May reduce coherence

  • Higher risk of unusual or incorrect output

Example:

Prompt: “The sky is”

High temperature:
“The sky is a swirling canvas of molten sapphire dreams.”

Used for:

  • Creative writing

  • Brainstorming

  • Story generation


Intuition

Think of temperature as controlling “confidence”:

Low temperature → model plays safe
High temperature → model explores more possibilities

It does not change what the model knows.
It only changes how it samples from what it knows.


Important Clarification

Temperature:

  • Does not retrain the model

  • Does not change internal weights

  • Only affects sampling behavior at inference time

It modifies how sharply the model selects the next token.


Key Trade-Off

Low temperature:

  • High accuracy

  • Low diversity

High temperature:

  • High diversity

  • Lower reliability

Choosing temperature depends on the goal of generation.

Difference between top-k sampling and top-p sampling?

Why Sampling Is Needed

When a language model generates text, it produces a probability distribution over the entire vocabulary for the next token.

Instead of always picking the highest-probability token (greedy decoding), we often sample from this distribution to introduce diversity.

Top-k and top-p are two sampling strategies used to control randomness.


Top-k Sampling

Top-k sampling restricts the model to consider only the k most probable tokens at each step.

How It Works

  1. Sort tokens by probability.

  2. Keep only the top k tokens.

  3. Set all other token probabilities to zero.

  4. Renormalize the remaining probabilities.

  5. Sample from these k tokens.

Example

If k = 5:

Only the 5 highest-probability tokens are considered.

If k = 1:
This becomes greedy decoding (always pick highest probability).

Characteristics

  • Fixed number of candidate tokens.

  • Simple and easy to control.

  • Smaller k → more deterministic.

  • Larger k → more diverse.

Limitation

It does not consider how probability mass is distributed.

Example:

  • If one token has 90% probability, but k = 50,
    it still allows 49 unlikely tokens.


Top-p Sampling (Nucleus Sampling)

Top-p sampling selects the smallest set of tokens whose cumulative probability is at least p.

Instead of fixing the number of tokens, it fixes the total probability mass.

How It Works

  1. Sort tokens by probability (descending).

  2. Add tokens one by one until cumulative probability ≥ p.

  3. Keep only that subset.

  4. Renormalize.

  5. Sample from this subset.

Example

If p = 0.9:

  • Select the smallest number of tokens whose probabilities sum to 90%.

  • Sometimes this may be 2 tokens.

  • Sometimes it may be 15 tokens.

  • It depends on how sharp the distribution is.

Characteristics

  • Dynamic number of candidate tokens.

  • Adapts to probability distribution shape.

  • Often produces more natural text.


Key Differences

  1. Selection Criterion

Top-k:
Selects a fixed number of tokens (k).

Top-p:
Selects a variable number of tokens based on cumulative probability threshold (p).


  1. Flexibility

Top-k:
Rigid — always keeps exactly k tokens.

Top-p:
Adaptive — number of tokens changes depending on distribution.


  1. Probability Awareness

Top-k:
Does not consider how probability mass is distributed.

Top-p:
Directly considers probability mass.


  1. Behavior in Sharp vs Flat Distributions

Sharp distribution (one token dominates):

  • Top-k may still allow many unlikely tokens.

  • Top-p may allow only a few tokens.

Flat distribution:

  • Top-k still keeps k tokens.

  • Top-p may include many tokens.


Practical Usage

Top-k:

  • Useful when strict control over randomness is needed.

  • Easier to reason about computationally.

Top-p:

  • More commonly used in modern LLM systems.

  • Produces better balance between coherence and diversity.

Often, temperature is used together with top-k or top-p to fine-tune generation behavior.

How does Adaptive Softmax speed up LLMs?

Why Softmax Is Expensive in LLMs

In language models, the final layer predicts a probability distribution over the entire vocabulary.

If the vocabulary size is large (e.g., 50,000–100,000 tokens), then for every token prediction, the model must:

  1. Compute logits for all vocabulary tokens.

  2. Apply softmax over the full vocabulary.

  3. Normalize across all tokens.

This is computationally expensive because:

  • The output layer is very large.

  • Most tokens are rarely used.

  • Every prediction requires full-vocabulary computation.

In large models, this becomes a major bottleneck.


What Is Adaptive Softmax?

Adaptive Softmax is an optimization technique designed to make softmax computation faster when dealing with large vocabularies.

The key idea:

Not all words are equally frequent.

Most natural language follows a Zipfian distribution:

  • A small number of words appear very frequently.

  • Most words appear rarely.

Adaptive Softmax exploits this imbalance.


Core Idea of Adaptive Softmax

Instead of treating all vocabulary tokens equally, it:

  1. Divides vocabulary into clusters based on frequency.

  2. Places frequent words in a small, fast-access cluster.

  3. Places rare words in larger clusters.

  4. Uses a hierarchical structure to reduce computation.

So instead of computing probabilities for all words at once, the model:

  • First predicts which cluster a word belongs to.

  • Then computes probabilities only within that cluster.


How It Works Conceptually

Imagine vocabulary divided into:

  • Head cluster → Most frequent words

  • Tail clusters → Less frequent words

Prediction steps:

  1. Compute probability over:

    • Head words

    • Cluster IDs (representing rare word groups)

  2. If prediction falls into a tail cluster:

    • Compute softmax only inside that cluster.

This reduces the number of computations significantly.


Why This Speeds Up Training

  1. Frequent words are handled efficiently
    Since most predictions involve frequent words, computation stays small.

  2. Rare words require extra computation only when needed
    Tail clusters are computed less often.

  3. Smaller matrix multiplications
    Instead of multiplying with the full vocabulary matrix, the model uses smaller projections for clusters.

This reduces:

  • Memory usage

  • Computation cost

  • Training time


Where Adaptive Softmax Is Most Useful

  • Very large vocabularies (100K+ tokens)

  • Training large language models

  • Situations where output layer dominates computation cost

It is more commonly used in training rather than inference.


Trade-Offs

Advantages:

  • Faster training

  • Lower memory consumption

  • Efficient for large vocabularies

Disadvantages:

  • More complex implementation

  • Slight approximation compared to full softmax

  • Less common in modern very large transformer LLMs that rely heavily on hardware acceleration


Key Insight

Adaptive Softmax speeds up LLM training by organizing the vocabulary hierarchically based on word frequency and avoiding full softmax computation over the entire vocabulary at every step.

What is cross-entropy loss and why is it used in language models?

What Is Cross-Entropy Loss?

Cross-entropy loss is a loss function used to measure how well a model’s predicted probability distribution matches the true distribution.

In classification problems, including language modeling, the model predicts probabilities over multiple classes (tokens), and cross-entropy measures the difference between:

  • The true distribution (ground truth)

  • The predicted distribution (model output)


Cross-Entropy in Language Models

In a language model, at each step:

  • The model predicts a probability distribution over the vocabulary.

  • The correct next token is known during training.

Example:

Vocabulary: {“cat”, “dog”, “bird”}
True next token: “dog”

True distribution (one-hot):

[0, 1, 0]

Predicted distribution:

[0.2, 0.6, 0.2]

Cross-entropy measures how close the predicted distribution is to the true distribution.


Mathematical Definition

For one training example:

Loss = − Σ yᵢ log(pᵢ)

Where:

  • yᵢ = true distribution (usually one-hot encoded)

  • pᵢ = predicted probability

  • log = natural logarithm

Since y is one-hot, only the correct class contributes:

Loss = −log(p_correct)

So if the correct token has:

  • High probability → low loss

  • Low probability → high loss


Example Calculation

If the model predicts:

Correct token probability = 0.9

Loss = −log(0.9) ≈ 0.105 (low loss)

If the model predicts:

Correct token probability = 0.01

Loss = −log(0.01) ≈ 4.605 (high loss)

This strongly penalizes confident wrong predictions.


Why Is Cross-Entropy Used in Language Models?

1. Works with Probabilities

Language models output probability distributions using softmax.

Cross-entropy naturally measures the difference between probability distributions.


2. Encourages High Confidence on Correct Tokens

The loss directly penalizes low probability assigned to the correct token.

The model learns to:

  • Increase probability of correct words

  • Decrease probability of incorrect words


3. Differentiable and Smooth

Cross-entropy is:

  • Continuous

  • Differentiable

This allows efficient gradient-based optimization using backpropagation.


4. Equivalent to Maximum Likelihood Training

Minimizing cross-entropy is equivalent to maximizing the likelihood of the training data.

Language modeling objective:

Maximize:

P(x₁, x₂, x₃, …, xₙ)

Cross-entropy loss implements this mathematically.


5. Proper Scoring Rule

Cross-entropy properly rewards well-calibrated probability distributions.

It does not just care about the correct token — it considers the entire distribution.


Cross-Entropy and Perplexity

In language models, performance is often measured using perplexity.

Perplexity = exp(cross-entropy)

Lower cross-entropy → lower perplexity → better model.


Summary of Why It Is Used

Cross-entropy loss is used in language models because it:

  • Measures how well predicted probabilities match true tokens

  • Strongly penalizes incorrect confident predictions

  • Is mathematically aligned with maximum likelihood training

  • Works naturally with softmax outputs

  • Enables stable gradient-based optimization

How are gradients computed with respect to embeddings?

1. Where Do Embeddings Fit in the Model?

In a Transformer or LLM, the pipeline looks like this:

Text → Tokenization → Token IDs → Embedding Lookup → Transformer Layers → Loss (Cross-Entropy)

Embeddings are the first trainable layer in the model.

Each token ID maps to a row in the embedding matrix:

Embedding Matrix:
Shape = (vocab_size × embedding_dim)

If vocab_size = 50,000 and embedding_dim = 768,
then the embedding matrix has 50,000 rows.

Each row is the vector representation of a token.


2. How Is the Loss Connected to Embeddings?

The loss (usually cross-entropy) depends on:

  • Final logits

  • Which depend on transformer layers

  • Which depend on input embeddings

So embeddings influence the final loss indirectly through the network.

To update embeddings, we use backpropagation.


3. Backpropagation to Embeddings (Conceptually)

Training process:

  1. Forward pass:

    • Token IDs → embeddings → transformer → logits → loss

  2. Backward pass:

    • Compute gradient of loss w.r.t. logits

    • Propagate gradients backward through all transformer layers

    • Eventually compute gradient w.r.t. embedding vectors

Mathematically, we compute:

∂Loss / ∂Embedding

This tells us:

“How should this embedding vector change to reduce the loss?”


4. Important Detail: Embedding Lookup Is Just Matrix Indexing

Embedding lookup works like:

Embedding(token_id) = embedding_matrix[token_id]

During backpropagation:

  • Only the rows corresponding to tokens in the batch receive gradients.

  • Other rows remain unchanged.

This makes embedding updates sparse.


5. How the Gradient Is Computed

Using the chain rule:

∂Loss / ∂Embedding
= ∂Loss / ∂Hidden₁ × ∂Hidden₁ / ∂Embedding

Where:

  • Hidden₁ is the output of the first transformer layer.

  • Gradients flow backward through all layers until reaching embeddings.

Autograd frameworks (PyTorch, TensorFlow) handle this automatically.


6. What Happens During Update?

After computing:

Gradient for embedding vector e_i

We update using gradient descent:

e_i ← e_i − learning_rate × gradient

This adjusts the embedding vector slightly.

Over many training steps:

  • Words that appear in similar contexts get similar updates.

  • Embeddings gradually capture semantic meaning.


7. Why This Works

Because:

  • The loss measures prediction error.

  • If the model predicts the wrong next word,
    gradients tell embeddings how to shift
    to improve prediction next time.

Example:

Sentence:
“The cat sat on the ___”

If model incorrectly predicts “dog” instead of “mat”:

  • Gradients push embeddings of:

    • “cat”

    • “sat”

    • “on”

  • In directions that increase probability of “mat” in similar contexts.


8. Special Case: Shared Embeddings

In many LLMs:

  • Input embeddings

  • Output softmax weights

Are tied (shared).

This means:

The same embedding matrix is used both:

  • At input

  • At output projection

In that case:

Gradients come from both:

  • Input pathway

  • Output softmax pathway

This improves parameter efficiency and learning stability.


9. Key Properties

  • Embedding gradients are sparse (only used tokens updated).

  • Computed via standard backpropagation.

  • Directly influenced by prediction error.

  • Learned end-to-end with the model.


Gradients with respect to embeddings are computed through backpropagation by applying the chain rule from the loss back to the embedding layer. Only the embedding vectors corresponding to tokens present in the batch receive updates, and these updates gradually shape embeddings to capture semantic and contextual meaning.

What is the role of the Jacobian matrix in backpropagation?

1. What Is the Jacobian Matrix?

The Jacobian matrix represents all first-order partial derivatives of a vector-valued function.

If we have a function:

y = f(x)

Where:

  • x is a vector of size n

  • y is a vector of size m

Then the Jacobian J is an m × n matrix:

J[i, j] = ∂yᵢ / ∂xⱼ

It tells us:

“How does each output component change with respect to each input component?”


2. Why Is the Jacobian Important in Neural Networks?

Neural networks are compositions of functions:

x → Layer1 → Layer2 → Layer3 → Loss

Each layer transforms a vector into another vector.

Backpropagation computes:

∂Loss / ∂Parameters

To do that, we repeatedly apply the chain rule.

For vector-valued functions, the chain rule uses Jacobian matrices.


3. Jacobian and the Chain Rule

Suppose:

z = f(x)
y = g(z)

We want:

∂y / ∂x

Using the multivariable chain rule:

∂y / ∂x = (∂y / ∂z) × (∂z / ∂x)

Here:

  • ∂z / ∂x is a Jacobian

  • ∂y / ∂z is another Jacobian

Backpropagation multiplies Jacobians layer by layer.


4. Example: Single Neural Network Layer

Consider:

z = Wx + b
a = activation(z)

Forward pass:
x → z → a

During backpropagation:

We compute:

∂Loss / ∂x

Using chain rule:

∂Loss / ∂x
= (∂Loss / ∂a) × (∂a / ∂z) × (∂z / ∂x)

Here:

  • ∂a / ∂z is a Jacobian (derivative of activation)

  • ∂z / ∂x = W

  • Matrix multiplications combine them

This is exactly Jacobian-based propagation.


5. In Deep Networks

In deep networks:

Layer L → Layer L−1 → ... → Input

Backprop computes gradients by multiplying Jacobians of each layer:

∂Loss / ∂x
= J_L × J_{L−1} × ... × J_1

Where each J is the Jacobian of one layer.

This sequential multiplication explains:

  • Vanishing gradients (Jacobians < 1 repeatedly multiplied)

  • Exploding gradients (Jacobians > 1 repeatedly multiplied)


6. Why We Don’t Explicitly Compute Full Jacobians

In large networks:

  • Jacobians are huge matrices.

  • Computing full matrices is computationally expensive.

Instead, modern frameworks compute:

Jacobian-vector products efficiently.

Backpropagation avoids building full Jacobians and directly computes:

∂Loss / ∂Parameters

Using efficient automatic differentiation.


7. In Attention and Transformers

Even in Transformers:

  • Softmax derivatives involve Jacobians.

  • Matrix multiplications use Jacobian relationships.

  • Gradient flow through attention uses Jacobian structure.

But again, these are handled implicitly by autograd systems.


8. Key Role of the Jacobian

The Jacobian enables:

  • Applying the multivariable chain rule

  • Propagating gradients backward

  • Understanding how small input changes affect outputs

  • Explaining gradient stability (vanishing/exploding)

Backpropagation is fundamentally repeated multiplication of Jacobians across layers.


The Jacobian matrix represents how outputs change with respect to inputs in vector-valued functions, and in backpropagation it enables the application of the multivariable chain rule to compute gradients through each layer of a neural network.

What is the vanishing gradient problem, and how do Transformers solve it?

What Is the Vanishing Gradient Problem?

The vanishing gradient problem occurs during backpropagation when gradients become extremely small as they propagate backward through many layers.

When gradients shrink toward zero:

  • Early layers receive almost no updates.

  • Learning becomes very slow.

  • Deep networks fail to train effectively.


Why Does It Happen?

During backpropagation, gradients are computed using repeated multiplication (chain rule).

For a deep network:

∂Loss / ∂Input
= J_L × J_{L−1} × ... × J_1

If many derivatives in these Jacobians are less than 1 (for example 0.1 or 0.01), multiplying them repeatedly causes:

0.1 × 0.1 × 0.1 × ... → very small number

In deep RNNs:

  • Gradients must pass through many time steps.

  • Sigmoid or tanh activations shrink gradients.

  • Long sequences worsen the issue.

Result:

  • Early layers (or early time steps in RNNs) barely learn.

  • Long-range dependencies are hard to capture.


Why It Was Severe in RNN-Based Seq2Seq Models

In RNNs:

  • Information flows sequentially.

  • Each time step depends on the previous hidden state.

  • Gradients must travel through many steps.

This creates long chains of multiplications, increasing the chance of vanishing gradients.

Even LSTMs reduce but do not fully eliminate the issue.


How Transformers Solve the Vanishing Gradient Problem

Transformers address this problem in several architectural ways.


1. No Recurrence (No Long Gradient Chains)

RNN:
Token 1 → Token 2 → Token 3 → ... → Token n

Transformer:
All tokens processed in parallel using self-attention.

In Transformers:

  • Any token can directly attend to any other token.

  • Dependency path length between tokens is 1 attention step.

This eliminates long chains of gradient multiplications across time.


2. Residual Connections

Each Transformer layer uses residual (skip) connections:

Output = Layer(x) + x

This allows gradients to flow directly across layers.

Even if the layer transformation shrinks gradients, the residual path preserves gradient flow.

This significantly reduces vanishing gradients in deep stacks.


3. Layer Normalization

Transformers use layer normalization to:

  • Stabilize activations

  • Prevent extreme scaling

  • Keep gradient magnitudes stable

This improves gradient flow during training.


4. Scaled Attention Mechanism

In attention:

Attention(Q, K, V) = softmax((QKᵀ) / √d_k) V

The scaling factor √d_k prevents extremely large values before softmax.

This stabilizes gradients and prevents numerical instability.


5. Better Activation Functions

Transformers typically use:

  • ReLU

  • GELU

These avoid strong gradient shrinking behavior seen in sigmoid/tanh.


Result

Because of:

  • Parallel computation

  • Short dependency paths

  • Residual connections

  • Layer normalization

  • Stable activation functions

Transformers train much deeper models without suffering from severe vanishing gradients.

This is one major reason why:

  • Very deep RNNs were hard to scale.

  • Very deep Transformers (hundreds of layers) are feasible.


Key Insight

The vanishing gradient problem happens when gradients shrink exponentially during backpropagation, especially in deep or recurrent networks. Transformers mitigate this by removing recurrence, using residual connections, normalization, and attention mechanisms that allow direct information flow across tokens.

What are eigenvalues and eigenvectors (in dimensionality reduction)?

1. What Are Eigenvalues and Eigenvectors?

Eigenvalues and eigenvectors are mathematical concepts from linear algebra that describe how a matrix transforms space.

If we have a matrix A, an eigenvector v satisfies:

A v = λ v

Where:

  • v is the eigenvector

  • λ (lambda) is the eigenvalue

  • A is a transformation matrix

This means:

When matrix A transforms vector v, the direction of v does not change — only its magnitude changes by a factor of λ.

So:

  • Eigenvector → Direction that stays the same after transformation

  • Eigenvalue → How much that direction is stretched or compressed


2. Geometric Intuition

Imagine a 2D shape being stretched or rotated.

Most vectors change direction when transformed.

But some special vectors:

  • Keep the same direction

  • Only change length

Those special vectors are eigenvectors.

The amount of stretching is given by eigenvalues.

If:

λ > 1 → stretched
0 < λ < 1 → compressed
λ = 0 → collapsed
λ < 0 → direction reversed


3. Why Are They Important in Dimensionality Reduction?

In dimensionality reduction (like PCA — Principal Component Analysis), eigenvectors and eigenvalues help us find:

  • The most important directions in the data

  • The directions where variance is maximum


4. Connection to PCA

Suppose we have a dataset with many features.

Goal:
Reduce dimensions while preserving as much information as possible.

Steps in PCA:

  1. Compute the covariance matrix of the data.

  2. Compute eigenvalues and eigenvectors of the covariance matrix.

  3. Sort eigenvectors by descending eigenvalues.

  4. Select top k eigenvectors.

  5. Project data onto those eigenvectors.


5. Why Does This Work?

In PCA:

  • Eigenvectors represent directions in feature space.

  • Eigenvalues represent how much variance exists in that direction.

Large eigenvalue → High variance → Important direction
Small eigenvalue → Low variance → Less important

By selecting top eigenvectors (largest eigenvalues), we:

  • Keep most of the data variability.

  • Remove less informative dimensions.


6. Example

Suppose you have 2D data shaped like an ellipse.

Most variance is along the long axis of the ellipse.

PCA finds:

  • First eigenvector → Long axis direction

  • Second eigenvector → Short axis direction

Eigenvalue for first axis is larger.

If you reduce from 2D → 1D:

You keep only the first eigenvector.

That preserves maximum variance.


7. Why This Reduces Dimensionality

Instead of using original features:

x₁, x₂, x₃, x₄, ...

We transform data into:

PC₁, PC₂, PC₃, ...

These principal components are eigenvectors.

If first 2 components capture 95% variance:

We can drop the rest.


8. In Matrix Terms

If covariance matrix = C

Solve:

C v = λ v

Where:

  • v = eigenvector (principal component direction)

  • λ = variance along that direction


9. Key Insight for Interviews

Eigenvectors identify the most important directions in data.
Eigenvalues tell us how important those directions are.

In dimensionality reduction:

We keep directions (eigenvectors) with largest eigenvalues to preserve maximum variance while reducing dimensions.


Eigenvalues measure variance magnitude along a direction.
Eigenvectors define the direction itself.

How is KL divergence used in evaluating LLMs?

1. What Is KL Divergence?

KL divergence (Kullback–Leibler divergence) measures how one probability distribution differs from another.

If we have:

  • True distribution: P

  • Model distribution: Q

KL divergence is:

KL(P || Q) = Σ P(x) log (P(x) / Q(x))

It measures how much information is lost when Q is used to approximate P.

Important:
KL divergence is not symmetric.

KL(P || Q) ≠ KL(Q || P)


2. Why KL Divergence Is Relevant in LLMs

LLMs output probability distributions over tokens.

At each step, the model predicts:

Q(token | context)

But during evaluation or training, we often compare:

  • A target distribution (P)

  • The model’s predicted distribution (Q)

KL divergence measures how close the model is to the target distribution.


3. Relationship Between KL Divergence and Cross-Entropy

In language modeling:

We usually train using cross-entropy loss.

Cross-entropy = KL divergence + entropy of true distribution.

When the true distribution is one-hot (only one correct token):

Minimizing cross-entropy is equivalent to minimizing KL divergence.

So in standard next-token prediction:

Cross-entropy training is implicitly minimizing KL divergence between:

  • True token distribution

  • Model distribution


4. KL Divergence in Model Evaluation

A. Comparing Two Models

Suppose we have:

  • Reference model P (e.g., pretrained model)

  • Fine-tuned model Q

We compute:

KL(P || Q)

This tells us how much the new model diverges from the original.

Used in:

  • Fine-tuning stability checks

  • Regularization during RLHF


B. In Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF)

KL divergence is explicitly used as a penalty.

During RLHF:

  • A reward model encourages certain behaviors.

  • But we don’t want the model to drift too far from the base model.

So training objective includes:

Reward − β × KL divergence

This ensures:

  • Model improves behavior

  • But does not collapse or become unstable

KL penalty prevents extreme shifts.


C. Measuring Distribution Shift

If input data changes (domain shift):

We can compare:

  • Distribution of tokens under training data

  • Distribution under new data

KL divergence measures how much they differ.

Used in:

  • Domain adaptation

  • Drift detection


5. In Knowledge Distillation

When compressing a large model into a smaller model:

  • Teacher model produces soft probability distribution P

  • Student model produces Q

We minimize:

KL(P || Q)

This allows the student to mimic teacher behavior.

Soft targets contain more information than one-hot labels.


6. Intuition in LLM Context

If:

  • KL divergence is small → model predictions are close to target distribution.

  • KL divergence is large → model behaves very differently.

In evaluation:

Lower KL divergence means the model better matches the reference distribution.


7. Key Uses of KL Divergence in LLMs

  1. Implicitly minimized through cross-entropy training

  2. Explicitly used in RLHF as regularization

  3. Used in knowledge distillation

  4. Used to measure divergence between model distributions

  5. Used for domain shift analysis


KL divergence in LLMs is primarily used to measure how close a model’s predicted probability distribution is to a target distribution, and it plays a central role in training, regularization (especially in RLHF), and model comparison.

What is LoRA and QLoRA?

What Is LoRA?

LoRA stands for Low-Rank Adaptation.

It is a parameter-efficient fine-tuning technique used to adapt large language models without updating all their weights.

Instead of fine-tuning the entire model (which may have billions of parameters), LoRA:

  • Freezes the original model weights.

  • Injects small trainable low-rank matrices into specific layers (usually attention layers).

  • Trains only those small matrices.


Why LoRA Is Needed

Fine-tuning full LLMs is expensive because:

  • Requires large GPU memory.

  • Requires storing full model gradients.

  • Computationally heavy.

LoRA drastically reduces:

  • Trainable parameters.

  • Memory usage.

  • Training cost.


How LoRA Works (Conceptually)

In a Transformer layer, we have weight matrices like:

W (for example in attention projection).

Normally, during fine-tuning:

W ← W − learning_rate × gradient

In LoRA:

  • Keep W frozen.

  • Add a low-rank update ΔW.

Instead of learning full ΔW (same size as W), LoRA approximates:

ΔW = A × B

Where:

  • A is (d × r)

  • B is (r × d)

  • r is small (rank, e.g., 4, 8, 16)

Since r << d:

This drastically reduces parameters.

Final weight becomes:

W' = W + A B

Only A and B are trained.


Why Low-Rank?

Large weight updates often lie in a low-dimensional subspace.

LoRA assumes:

We don’t need to update the full matrix.
We only need to learn a low-rank adjustment.

This works surprisingly well in practice.


Benefits of LoRA

  1. Massive reduction in trainable parameters

  2. Lower GPU memory usage

  3. Faster fine-tuning

  4. Can store multiple adapters for different tasks

  5. No change to base model weights


What Is QLoRA?

QLoRA stands for Quantized Low-Rank Adaptation.

It extends LoRA by adding quantization.

Instead of storing the base model in full precision (e.g., 16-bit or 32-bit), QLoRA:

  • Quantizes the base model to 4-bit precision.

  • Keeps LoRA adapters in higher precision.

  • Trains efficiently on consumer GPUs.


How QLoRA Works

  1. Load pretrained model in 4-bit quantized format.

  2. Freeze quantized weights.

  3. Add LoRA adapters.

  4. Train only LoRA parameters.

  5. Use special techniques to maintain numerical stability.

This enables fine-tuning very large models (e.g., 65B parameters) on limited hardware.


Why QLoRA Is Powerful

  • Reduces memory dramatically.

  • Keeps training stable.

  • Allows large-model fine-tuning on single GPUs.

  • Makes LLM fine-tuning accessible.


Difference Between LoRA and QLoRA

LoRA:

  • Freezes full-precision model.

  • Adds low-rank trainable adapters.

  • Reduces number of trainable parameters.

QLoRA:

  • Quantizes base model to 4-bit.

  • Uses LoRA adapters for training.

  • Further reduces memory usage.

LoRA = Parameter-efficient fine-tuning
QLoRA = Parameter-efficient + Memory-efficient fine-tuning


Where They Are Used

  • Instruction tuning

  • Domain adaptation

  • Chatbot customization

  • Fine-tuning open-source LLMs

  • Research experimentation


LoRA and QLoRA enable efficient adaptation of large language models without full fine-tuning, dramatically reducing computational and memory requirements.

How can catastrophic forgetting be mitigated in LLMs?

What Is Catastrophic Forgetting?

Catastrophic forgetting occurs when a model forgets previously learned knowledge after being fine-tuned on new data.

In LLMs, this typically happens when:

  • A pretrained model is fine-tuned on a narrow domain.

  • The new training data is small or highly specialized.

  • Model weights shift too far from the original distribution.

Result:

  • Performance improves on the new task.

  • Performance degrades on general tasks.


Why It Happens

Neural networks learn by updating weights.

During fine-tuning:

  • Gradients are computed only from new data.

  • Model adapts strongly toward new objective.

  • Old knowledge may be overwritten.

Because LLMs share parameters across tasks, updates for one task affect all behaviors.


Methods to Mitigate Catastrophic Forgetting

1. Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (LoRA, Adapters)

Instead of updating all model weights:

  • Freeze base model.

  • Train small adapter layers (LoRA, adapters).

Benefits:

  • Base knowledge preserved.

  • Only task-specific changes are learned.

  • Easy to switch between tasks.

This is one of the most practical solutions.


2. Regularization Toward Original Weights

Add a penalty that discourages large deviations from original weights.

Example:

Loss = Task Loss + λ × ||W − W_original||²

This keeps weights close to pretrained values.

Used in continual learning methods like:

  • Elastic Weight Consolidation (EWC)

EWC penalizes changes to important parameters more heavily.


3. KL-Divergence Regularization (Used in RLHF)

Encourage new model distribution to stay close to base model distribution.

Objective:

New Loss − β × KL(new_model || base_model)

This ensures:

  • Behavioral improvements

  • Without drifting too far

Widely used in RLHF training.


4. Replay or Mixed Training Data

Train on:

  • New task data

  • A subset of original/general data

This reinforces previous knowledge while learning new task.

Called:

  • Rehearsal

  • Experience replay

Effective but requires access to old data.


5. Multi-Task Learning

Train simultaneously on multiple tasks.

Instead of sequential fine-tuning:

Train on combined objectives.

Helps model maintain broad knowledge.


6. Low Learning Rate

Using a smaller learning rate:

  • Reduces drastic weight updates.

  • Preserves pretrained knowledge.

  • Makes adaptation more stable.


7. Freezing Lower Layers

Lower layers often capture general language features.

Strategy:

  • Freeze early layers.

  • Fine-tune only higher layers.

Preserves core linguistic knowledge.


8. Continual Learning Techniques

Advanced approaches:

  • Progressive networks

  • Regularization-based continual learning

  • Memory-based approaches

More common in research than production LLM systems.


Practical Strategy in LLM Fine-Tuning

Most commonly used methods:

  • LoRA / QLoRA

  • KL regularization

  • Mixed data training

  • Small learning rates

These provide a balance between adaptation and knowledge retention.


Key Insight

Catastrophic forgetting happens because fine-tuning updates shared parameters in ways that overwrite prior knowledge. It can be mitigated by freezing base weights, using adapter-based training, regularization, replaying old data, and controlling distribution drift through KL penalties.

How does Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) prevent catastrophic forgetting?

What Is Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT)?

PEFT methods (like LoRA, Adapters, Prefix Tuning, Prompt Tuning):

  • Freeze the original pretrained model weights.

  • Add small trainable components.

  • Train only those new parameters.

The base model remains unchanged.


How PEFT Prevents Catastrophic Forgetting

1. Freezing the Base Model

The most important reason:

Original weights are not updated.

Since catastrophic forgetting occurs due to weight overwriting, freezing the weights eliminates that risk.

Base knowledge remains intact.


2. Learning Task-Specific Adjustments

PEFT methods introduce small modules that learn:

Task-specific transformations

For example in LoRA:

W' = W + A B

Where:

  • W is frozen

  • A and B are small trainable matrices

The original representation is preserved, and only a small adjustment is learned.


3. Isolation of Task Knowledge

Each task can have its own adapter.

This allows:

  • Switching between tasks

  • No interference between tasks

  • Modular knowledge storage

Instead of one model forgetting old tasks, you attach different adapters.


4. Reduced Parameter Drift

Full fine-tuning modifies millions or billions of parameters.

PEFT modifies only a small subset.

Smaller parameter updates → lower risk of destabilizing the model.


5. Better Stability with Limited Data

When fine-tuning on small datasets:

  • Full fine-tuning may overfit.

  • PEFT restricts learning capacity.

  • Reduces over-adjustment.

This preserves generalization ability.


Example

Suppose a base model knows:

  • General English

  • Science

  • Coding

  • History

You fine-tune on medical text.

Full fine-tuning:
Model shifts heavily toward medical domain.
General knowledge may degrade.

PEFT:
Base model remains unchanged.
Medical adapter modifies behavior only when needed.
General knowledge remains accessible.


Why This Works Theoretically

LLMs already contain rich representations.

Often, new tasks require:

  • Slight shifts

  • Not full re-learning

PEFT assumes:

Task adaptation lies in a low-dimensional subspace.

So instead of rewriting the model, we learn a small correction.


Common PEFT Techniques

  • LoRA

  • QLoRA

  • Adapters

  • Prefix tuning

  • Prompt tuning

All share the same core idea:

Preserve pretrained weights and learn lightweight modifications.


Key Insight

Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning prevents catastrophic forgetting by freezing the original pretrained weights and learning small task-specific adjustments instead of modifying the entire model. This preserves previously learned knowledge while allowing adaptation to new tasks.

What is model distillation, and how is it applied to LLMs?

What is model distillation, and how is it applied to LLMs?

Model distillation is a way of making a smaller model learn from a bigger model.

Think of it like this:


A senior teacher (big model) understands everything deeply.
A junior student (small model) learns from the teacher’s explanations.


Instead of learning directly from textbooks (raw training data), the student learns from the teacher’s understanding.

That process is called model distillation.


Let’s understand why this is needed.

Large Language Models (LLMs) like GPT-style models are:

  • Very powerful

  • Very accurate

  • But very large

  • Expensive to run

  • Slow on small devices

Now imagine you want to run an AI model:

  • On a mobile phone

  • Inside a browser

  • On an edge device

  • Or in a low-cost production system

You cannot always afford a 100+ billion parameter model.

So what do we do?

We train a smaller model to behave like the large one.

That is model distillation.


Now let’s understand how it actually works.

Normally, when training a model, we use:

Input → Correct Output (hard labels)

Example:


Input: "2 + 2 = ?"
Output: "4"


But in distillation, we do something smarter.

Instead of using only correct answers, we use the teacher model’s output probabilities.

For example:

If we ask a teacher model:

"What is the capital of France?"

Instead of just answering:

"Paris"

It gives probabilities like:

  • Paris → 0.92

  • Lyon → 0.04

  • Marseille → 0.02

  • London → 0.01

These probabilities are called soft targets.

Why are they useful?

Because they contain extra knowledge.


The teacher is not just saying "Paris is correct".
It is also telling us:


  • Lyon is somewhat related

  • London is clearly wrong

This gives richer learning information.

The student model learns from these soft targets.


So in simple steps, distillation works like this:

  1. Train a large teacher model (already done in most cases)

  2. Pass training data through the teacher

  3. Collect the teacher’s output probabilities

  4. Train the smaller student model to match those outputs

The student tries to imitate the teacher’s behavior.


Now let’s talk specifically about LLMs.

In LLM distillation:

  • The teacher is a large LLM (like GPT-4-level models)

  • The student is a smaller transformer model

The student learns to:

  • Predict next tokens similar to the teacher

  • Generate similar responses

  • Follow instructions similarly

  • Maintain similar reasoning patterns (to some extent)

This is often done using:

  • Teacher-generated text

  • Teacher probability distributions

  • Sometimes chain-of-thought outputs


There are different types of distillation used in LLMs:


  1. Response-based distillation
    The student learns from final outputs of the teacher.



  2. Logit-based distillation
    The student learns from the raw probability scores (logits).



  3. Feature-based distillation
    The student tries to match internal layer representations of the teacher.



Let’s make it even simpler with an analogy.

Imagine a master chef and a trainee.

You could train the trainee by:


  • Giving recipe books (raw data)
    OR


  • Making them watch the master cook and copy technique

Watching the master cook includes:

  • Timing

  • Small adjustments

  • Hidden tricks

  • Judgment calls

That is like soft targets in distillation.

The trainee learns more efficiently.


Now why is distillation important for LLMs?

Because it helps in:

  • Reducing model size

  • Faster inference

  • Lower memory usage

  • Lower cost

  • Deployment on smaller hardware

Many small models you see today are distilled versions of larger models.

For example:

  • Distilled BERT (DistilBERT)

  • Smaller instruction-tuned LLMs trained from bigger ones


Let’s see a very small conceptual code example.

Suppose:


teacher_logits = [2.5, 0.3, -1.2]
student_logits = [2.0, 0.5, -0.8]


We convert them into probabilities using softmax.

Then we compute a loss between:


teacher probabilities
and
student probabilities


This loss is often called distillation loss.

The student minimizes this loss during training.

This means:

"Make your behavior closer to the teacher."


One more important concept: Temperature.

In distillation, we often use a parameter called temperature (T).

When T > 1:

  • Probabilities become softer

  • Differences between classes become smoother

Why?

Because very sharp probabilities (like 0.99 and 0.001) hide useful information.

Temperature makes them more spread out.

This helps the student learn better relationships.


Now let’s summarize clearly.

Model distillation is:


Training a small model (student)
to imitate a large model (teacher)
using soft probability outputs
instead of only correct answers.


In LLMs, it helps create:

  • Smaller

  • Faster

  • Cheaper

  • Deployment-friendly models

Without losing too much performance.

What is overfitting, and how can it be prevented?

Overfitting happens when a model memorizes the training data instead of learning general patterns.

Imagine a student preparing for an exam.

If the student memorizes only past question papers word by word, they may score well if the same questions appear again.

But if the questions are slightly different, they fail.

That is overfitting.

The model performs:

  • Very well on training data

  • Poorly on new, unseen data


Let’s understand with a simple example.

Suppose we are predicting house prices.

Training data:

  • Small house → $100k

  • Medium house → $200k

  • Large house → $300k

A good model learns:

"Bigger house → higher price"

But an overfitted model might learn:

  • 1200 sq ft house on Maple Street → $197,350

  • 1210 sq ft house on Oak Street → $203,920

It memorizes noise instead of learning the real rule.


Why does overfitting happen?

  • Model is too complex (too many parameters)

  • Not enough training data

  • Training too long

  • Too much noise in data


How can we prevent overfitting?

  1. More Data

More examples help the model see patterns clearly.

Like a student solving many types of problems.


  1. Regularization

This adds a penalty for very large weights.

Common types:

  • L1 Regularization

  • L2 Regularization

It forces the model to stay simple.

Think of it like telling the student:
"Don’t overcomplicate your answers."


  1. Dropout (in Neural Networks)

Randomly turns off some neurons during training.

This prevents the network from depending too much on specific paths.

Like asking a student to sometimes solve problems without certain hints.


  1. Early Stopping

Stop training when validation error starts increasing.

If training accuracy keeps improving but validation accuracy drops, that is a red flag.


  1. Cross-Validation

Split data into multiple parts and test properly.

This ensures the model generalizes well.

Let’s use a simple example.

  • Not Spam

Given the email, what is the label?

Example:

Generative models can also create new data.

For example:

  • Generate new images

  • Write text

  • Create music


Simple analogy:

Discriminative model:
A judge deciding if a painting is real or fake.

Generative model:
An artist who can paint in the same style.

How does Mixture of Experts (MoE) improve scalability?

How does Mixture of Experts (MoE) improve scalability?

Mixture of Experts (MoE) is a technique that allows a model to become very large and powerful without becoming very slow and expensive.

To understand this clearly, let’s start simple.

Imagine a hospital.

Instead of one super doctor who treats everything — heart problems, brain surgery, broken bones — the hospital has:

  • A heart specialist

  • A brain specialist

  • An orthopedic specialist

  • And many more

Now when a patient comes in, they don’t see all doctors.

They are sent only to the right specialist.

This makes the system:

  • More efficient

  • More scalable

  • Faster

That is exactly how Mixture of Experts works.


In a normal neural network:

Every input goes through all neurons in every layer.

If the model has 100 billion parameters, every token uses all 100 billion.

That is expensive.

Now imagine we split part of the model into multiple "experts".

Each expert is a separate neural network.

For example:

  • Expert 1 → good at math

  • Expert 2 → good at coding

  • Expert 3 → good at writing

  • Expert 4 → good at reasoning

Instead of activating all experts for every input, we use something called a gating network.

The gating network decides:

"Which experts should handle this token?"

Usually, it selects only 1 or 2 experts.

This is called sparse activation.


Here is the key idea:

Even if the model has 100 experts,
only 1 or 2 are used per token.

So:

Total parameters = Very large
Active parameters per token = Small

This is how MoE improves scalability.


Let’s break this down clearly.

In a dense model:

All parameters are used every time.

Computation cost grows directly with model size.

In MoE:

Total parameters can grow massively
But computation per token stays almost constant

This means:

You can increase model capacity
Without increasing compute proportionally

That is scalability.


Think of it like this:

Dense model = One giant factory where every worker works on every product.

MoE = Huge company with many departments, but only relevant departments handle each task.

More capacity.
Same compute per request.


Now let’s look at what happens technically.

In a transformer model, usually the Feed-Forward Network (FFN) layer is replaced with multiple experts.

Instead of:

Output = FFN(Input)

We do:

  1. Gating network computes scores for each expert

  2. Select top-k experts (for example, top 2)

  3. Route input to only those experts

  4. Combine their outputs

Small conceptual example:

experts = [E1, E2, E3, E4]

selected = gate(input)   # returns top 2 experts

output = E1(input) + E3(input)

Here:

  • We have 4 experts

  • Only 2 are used

  • Others remain inactive

This saves computation.


Now let’s connect this to scalability.

There are three main benefits.

  1. Increased Model Capacity

You can add more experts.

More experts = more total parameters.

But you don’t increase compute much per token.

So the model can store more knowledge.


  1. Efficient Training

Different experts specialize in different patterns.

Some become good at:

  • Code

  • Math

  • Language

  • Facts

This specialization improves performance.


  1. Better Compute-Performance Tradeoff

In traditional scaling:

More parameters = more FLOPs (compute cost).

In MoE:

More parameters ≠ proportional increase in FLOPs.

This breaks the traditional scaling limit.

That is why very large modern LLMs (like Switch Transformer, Mixtral, etc.) use MoE.


But there are challenges too:

  • Load balancing (some experts may get too much traffic)

  • Complex routing

  • Distributed training difficulty

Still, the scalability benefit is huge.

What is a context window?

A context window is the amount of text a language model can read and remember at one time.

Think of it like short-term memory.

If I give you a long paragraph and ask a question about the first sentence, you can answer only if you still remember it.

LLMs work the same way.

They can only “see” a limited number of tokens at once.


What is a token?

A token is a small piece of text.

It could be:

  • A word

  • Part of a word

  • A punctuation mark

For example:

“ChatGPT is powerful.”

Might be split into tokens like:

  • Chat

  • GPT

  • is

  • powerful

  • .


If a model has a context window of 8,000 tokens, it means:

It can look at the last 8,000 tokens of conversation.

If the conversation becomes longer than that, older parts are removed from memory.


Real-life analogy:

Imagine a whiteboard that can only hold 8,000 words.

When it becomes full, you must erase the oldest content to write new content.

That is how context windows work.


Why is this important?

Because it affects:

  • Long conversations

  • Document summarization

  • Code generation

  • Memory of previous instructions

A larger context window means the model can handle longer inputs without forgetting earlier details.

2026 update: Context windows of 128K–2M tokens are now standard — GPT-4o: 128K, Claude 3.5: 200K, Gemini 2.0: 1M+. The practical constraint has shifted from "will the model fit this input?" to cost management: sending more tokens costs more money, regardless of whether the model needs them all. Large context does not automatically mean good context — retrieval and context compression are often better than stuffing everything in.

It is chosen before training.

The model does not learn it automatically.

When training a model, there are two types of values:

  1. Parameters
    These are learned from data.
    Example: weights in a neural network.

  2. Hyperparameters
    These are set by the engineer.
    They control the training process.


Example:

If you are cooking rice:

  • The rice and water are like parameters (they change during cooking).

  • The flame level and cooking time are like hyperparameters.

You choose them before cooking.


Common hyperparameters:

  • Learning rate (how fast the model updates)

  • Batch size (how many samples per training step)

  • Number of epochs (how many times we see the data)

  • Number of layers

  • Dropout rate


Example:



learning_rate = 0.001
batch_size = 32
epochs = 10

These values are not learned.

They are chosen.

Because bad choices can cause:

  • Slow training

  • Overfitting

  • Underfitting

  • Training failure

In interviews, you can say:

A hyperparameter is a configuration value set before training that controls how a model learns, such as learning rate, batch size, or number of layers.

The Alignment Pipeline — From Base Model to Chat Assistant

How does a base language model become a helpful chat assistant?

Pretraining produces a base model — a powerful text predictor that continues any input probabilistically. Ask it "What is the capital of France?" and it might respond "What is the capital of Germany? What is the capital of Spain?" — it's predicting what text typically follows a geography question, not answering it.

The alignment pipeline transforms this into a chat assistant through three stages:

  • Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT): train on high-quality instruction-response examples
  • Reward Model Training: learn to predict which responses humans prefer
  • Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF): use the reward model to improve the policy via PPO, or skip to Direct Preference Optimization (DPO)

Each stage is necessary: SFT shapes format and style; RLHF/DPO shapes values and helpfulness.

What is Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) and what makes a good SFT dataset?

SFT trains the base model on (instruction, response) pairs — teaching it the format of helpful interaction. The dataset must be: high quality (well-written responses from expert humans), diverse (questions across many domains and task types), and balanced (mix of task types: QA, summarisation, coding, reasoning).

Key insight: SFT doesn't add new knowledge — it teaches the model to use its existing knowledge helpfully. A model with strong pretraining and weak SFT will underperform a model with average pretraining and excellent SFT data. OpenAI's InstructGPT used ~13K demonstration examples for SFT — tiny compared to pretraining.

What is a reward model in RLHF and what does it predict?

A reward model (RM) is a classifier trained on human preference data. Input: a prompt + a model response. Output: a scalar score representing how much a human rater would approve of this response. Trained on pairwise comparisons: "Given prompt P and responses A and B, which is better?"

Key failure mode: reward hacking — the policy (language model) learns to generate text that tricks the reward model into giving high scores, without actually being helpful. Example: verbose, confident, flattering responses score highly on naive RMs even when incorrect. Mitigation: use diverse annotators, iterate RM training as the policy improves.

What is Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) and why has it largely replaced PPO?

PPO-based RLHF requires maintaining four models simultaneously: the policy, reference policy, reward model, and value function. Training is unstable and sensitive to hyperparameters.

DPO (Rafailov et al., 2023) derives a direct objective from the same human preference data, eliminating the reward model and RL training loop entirely. The loss directly increases the log probability of preferred responses relative to rejected ones. In practice: DPO trains more stably, requires less memory, and achieves comparable or better alignment results than PPO. Most open-source aligned models (Llama-3-Instruct, Mistral-Instruct) use DPO-style training.

The trade-off: DPO may be less able to improve beyond the quality of the SFT model; PPO can theoretically explore beyond the initial behaviour.

What is Constitutional AI (CAI) and why does it matter for scaling alignment?

Human annotation is the bottleneck in RLHF — trained annotators are expensive and slow. Constitutional AI (Anthropic, 2022) addresses this with AI Feedback (RLAIF): instead of human raters, use the AI itself to generate preference data.

The "constitution" is a set of principles ("choose the response that is less harmful", "choose the more honest response"). Process:

  • Generate responses
  • Ask the model to critique each response against the constitution
  • Ask it to revise based on the critique
  • Use the (original, revised) pairs as preference data for DPO/RLHF

Why it scales: removes the human annotation bottleneck while maintaining principled guidance. Used in training Claude (Anthropic). Limitation: RLAIF inherits the biases of the base model used as judge.
